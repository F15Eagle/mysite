## Summary
In 2025, I planned and executed my Eagle Scout Service Project benefiting [Evanston Grows](https://www.evanstongrows.org/), a local urban farming nonprofit that provides fresh produce to communities living in food deserts. Specifically, I engineered and built a 110-cubic-foot composter to process organic waste at their Faith Temple farm site.

The entire process closely mirrored an engineering lifecycle, requiring multiple stages of rigorous approval, design reviews, and final presentations. Throughout the experience, I learned an enormous amount about the critical importance of robust planning—especially accounting for contingency scenarios—as well as team leadership, proactive communication, and community coordination. 

I am incredibly grateful to Scout Troop 924, my friends, and my family for generously supporting me with their time and funding throughout this journey.

> ### Stats:
> * $1000+ raised
> * 25+ volunteers
> * Over 210 man-hours of combined effort

## Motivation and Community Impact
Access to fresh, sustainable food has always been a core priority for me. Aside from simply being a passionate foodie, my family roots trace back to India—a region that faced severe, systemic food security challenges just a few decades ago before agricultural transformations took hold. Because of this background, I wanted my mandatory Eagle project to be more than a checkbox; I wanted it to leave a lasting, operational impact on local food equity. 

Partnering with Evanston Grows allowed me to apply engineering logic to community service. By building a massive, onsite 110-cubic-foot composter, the Faith Temple farm site gained the ability to infinitely reprocess its own organic agricultural waste. This closed-loop system fundamentally changed their unit economics, eliminating their reliance on commercial, yearly compost purchases. As a result, the non-profit can now permanently reallocate those recurring capital funds directly toward scaling up actual food production for underserved local neighborhoods.

## Managing Materials and Constraints
Even the best-laid plans encounter sudden roadblocks. Mid-way through the procurement phase, I ran into a critical material choice: whether to use standard, cost-effective treated construction lumber or switch to premium natural cedar. Treated lumber was affordable but carried chemical preservation agents that raised potential food-safety concerns for organic urban farming. Cedar was completely food-safe and durable, but its premium cost threatened to inflate my materials budget by nearly 70%.

Unwilling to compromise the integrity of the project's purpose, I reached out to my Scoutmaster for advice. His guidance was simple but foundational: *always prioritize quality and safety in the things you build for others.*

Accepting the constraint, I pivoted my strategy. I leaned heavily into fundraising and community outreach, successfully scaling our capital generation past the $1,000 mark to absorb the pricing spike. From there, I managed the intensive logistical paperwork and safety permits required to bring the raw materials to the farm site. 

On the construction days, I stepped into a purely managerial role, coordinating and leading a diverse workforce of 25 youth and adult volunteers. Over the course of 210 combined man-hours, we managed the layout, cutting, assembly, and secure anchoring of the structure. The final, completed cedar composter stands as proof that a person of action doesn't cut corners when faced with unexpected project constraints.
