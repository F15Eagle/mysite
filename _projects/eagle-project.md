## Summary
In 2025, I planned and executed my Eagle Scout Service Project benefiting [Evanston Grows](https://www.evanstongrows.org/), a local urban farming nonprofit that provides fresh produce to communities living in food deserts[cite: 1, 2]. Specifically, I engineered and built a 110-cubic-foot composter to process organic waste at their Faith Temple farm site[cite: 1, 2].

The entire process closely mirrored an engineering lifecycle, requiring multiple stages of rigorous approval, design reviews, and final presentations. Throughout the experience, I learned an enormous amount about the critical importance of robust planning—especially accounting for contingency scenarios—as well as team leadership, proactive communication, and community coordination. 

I am incredibly grateful to Scout Troop 924, my friends, and my family for generously supporting me with their time and funding throughout this journey[cite: 1].

> ### Stats:
> * $1000+ raised[cite: 2]
> * 25+ volunteers[cite: 1, 2]
> * Over 210 man-hours of combined effort[cite: 2]

## Motivation and Community Impact
Access to fresh, sustainable food has always been a core priority for me. Aside from simply being a passionate foodie, my family roots trace back to India—a region that faced severe, systemic food security challenges just a few decades ago before agricultural transformations took hold[cite: 2]. Because of this background, I wanted my mandatory Eagle project to be more than a checkbox; I wanted it to leave a lasting, operational impact on local food equity[cite: 2]. 

Partnering with Evanston Grows allowed me to apply engineering logic to community service[cite: 2]. By building a massive, onsite 110-cubic-foot composter, the Faith Temple farm site gained the ability to infinitely reprocess its own organic agricultural waste[cite: 1, 2]. This closed-loop system fundamentally changed their unit economics, eliminating their reliance on commercial, yearly compost purchases[cite: 2]. As a result, the non-profit can now permanently reallocate those recurring capital funds directly toward scaling up actual food production for underserved local neighborhoods[cite: 2].

## Managing Materials and Constraints
Even the best-laid plans encounter sudden roadblocks. Mid-way through the procurement phase, I ran into a critical material choice: whether to use standard, cost-effective treated construction lumber or switch to premium natural cedar[cite: 2]. Treated lumber was affordable but carried chemical preservation agents that raised potential food-safety concerns for organic urban farming[cite: 2]. Cedar was completely food-safe and durable, but its premium cost threatened to inflate my materials budget by nearly 70%[cite: 2].

Unwilling to compromise the integrity of the project's purpose, I reached out to my Scoutmaster for advice[cite: 2]. His guidance was simple but foundational: *always prioritize quality and safety in the things you build for others.*[cite: 2]

Accepting the constraint, I pivoted my strategy[cite: 2]. I leaned heavily into fundraising and community outreach, successfully scaling our capital generation past the $1,000 mark to absorb the pricing spike[cite: 2]. From there, I managed the intensive logistical paperwork and safety permits required to bring the raw materials to the farm site[cite: 2]. 

On the construction days, I stepped into a purely managerial role, coordinating and leading a diverse workforce of 25 youth and adult volunteers[cite: 1, 2]. Over the course of 210 combined man-hours, we managed the layout, cutting, assembly, and secure anchoring of the structure[cite: 2]. The final, completed cedar composter stands as proof that a person of action doesn't cut corners when faced with unexpected project constraints[cite: 2].
